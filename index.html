Toronto's
Tree Pests

Pest

A pest is a plant, fungus, or animal species that people consider damaging or harmful. An animal can be a pest when it causes damage to a wild ecosystem.

Invasive Pests

An invasive pest is a species that is not native to Toronto and which can spread to a degree believed to cause damage to the environment, economy or human health.

Urban Forest

Urban forestry is the planning, planting, protection, maintenance, and care of trees, forests, greenspace and resources in and around cities and communities for the benefit of people. Economic, environmental, social, and public health are benefits the urban forest provides.

Canopy

The canopy is the upper layer or habitat zone, formed by mature tree crowns and other biological organisms. The canopy photosynthesizes the quickest as it's closest to sunlight, providing oxygen for us to breathe.

Meet Toronto's
Invasive Insects

Toronto’s urban forest faces threats from multiple insect pests. Some of these have been introduced into Canada through global trade, including wood packaging from other countries.

Emerald Ash Borer

Origins:
Asia

Symptoms:
Winding trails in tree bark

The most immediate and significant threat to Toronto’s trees is the Emerald Ash Borer from Asia. It kills ash trees by feeding beneath the bark and disrupting the flow of water and nutrients within the tree. Since being discovered in Detroit in 2002, EAB has killed millions of ash trees in Southwestern Ontario.

Asian Longhorned Beetle

Origins:
Asia and Europe

Symptoms:
Dime sized holes in tree bark

The Asian Longhorned Beetle is an insect from Asia that bores into and kills a wide range of hardwood species. Since there is no known natural predator in North America, the ALHB poses as a serious threat with the potential to destroy up to 70% of the trees and canopy of the urban forest.

European Gypsy Moth

Origins:
Asia, Europe, and part of Africa

Symptoms:
Defoliation of tree leaves

The European Gypsy Moth is an introduced defoliating insect from Europe, Asia, and Africa that is considered to be a major pest in North America. The caterpillar eats the leaves of trees making them more susceptible to disease and damage from other insects.

Problems

1. Canopy Loss

Maintaining tree canopy in the urban forest is important because it provides social, ecological and economic benefits as well as reducing impacts of climate change, and increasing air quality and public health. These three invasive insects are a problem as they reduce tree canopy. The proportions relative to eachother are represented below.



The Asian Longhorned Beetle (red) represents a potential risk to 42.9% of the population.
The Gypsy Moth (blue) represents a potential risk to 16.2% of the population.
The Emerald Ash Borer represents a potential risk to 8.4% of the population.

2. Compensating Ecological Services ($ billions)

The urban forest has a structural value worth $7.1 billion. It provides over $28 million annually in ecological services, including climate change and air pollution mitigation and energy conservation benefits, plus additional storm water management services.

The ALB represents a loss 4.5 million trees and $4.0 billion in structural value.

Success

Eradication of the Asian Longhorned Beetle

The ALHB eradication program that began in September 2003 in the Greater Toronto Area. The City partnered with the Canadian Forest Service in the development of a branch sampling technique now widely utilized in the detection of EAB, an invasive insect known to attack ash trees. To date, the eradication effort has been successful with no viable life stages of the insect being detected.
